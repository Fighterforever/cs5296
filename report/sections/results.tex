\subsection{Steady-state throughput and latency}
\label{sec:results-steady}

\Cref{fig:steady} plots median, p95, and p99 latency on the GET-redirect
path against the target RPS of each k6 scenario stage. Three regimes are
visible. Lambda's trace is flat across the measured band: p50~$\approx$~29\,ms,
p95~$\approx$~40\,ms, p99~$\approx$~52\,ms from 25 up to 75\,RPS, with
percentile confidence intervals of $\pm$1\,ms on p95. The EC2 ASG holds a
13--21\,ms median all the way to 200\,RPS but its tail widens rapidly:
p95 climbs from 26\,ms at 50\,RPS through 617\,ms at 200\,RPS, with p99
reaching 2\,s long before target tracking completes a scale-out. By
400\,RPS the single t3.small is CPU-saturated and p50 itself is 3.5\,s.
Fargate with a single 0.5\,vCPU task shows the steepest degradation: a
clean 13\,ms p50 at 50\,RPS, then a cliff to 2.9\,s p50 at 100\,RPS. At
400\,RPS, 6.9\,\% of Fargate requests hit the 60\,s ALB idle timeout.

\subsection{Elasticity under a step-arrival burst}
\label{sec:results-elasticity}

\Cref{fig:elasticity-trace} shows per-second p95 for the burst scenario.
EC2 and Fargate were driven at a 400\,RPS target for three minutes;
Lambda was driven at a 75\,RPS target (again, the quota cap). Lambda
tracks its steady-state envelope throughout --- the p95 trace lies
within a narrow 35--60\,ms band from the first second of the burst to
the last. EC2 shows the expected on-ramp: the first minute of the burst
runs at a 2--4\,s p95 while the ASG's CloudWatch target-tracking metric
accumulates, then tapers as the second instance becomes healthy. Fargate
exhibits the most extreme response: the single pre-scaled task collapses
under load within seconds, and service autoscaling at 30\,s cooldown is
not fast enough to bring the p95 below one second within our burst
window.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/elasticity_p95_trace}
  \caption{Per-second p95 latency through pre-burst, burst (grey band),
    and post-burst phases. y-axis is log-scaled.}
  \label{fig:elasticity-trace}
\end{figure}

Our automatic time-to-steady-state estimator
(\cref{sec:methodology}) reports only sub-second differences between the
three paradigms because the steady p95 threshold is dominated by
Lambda's clean trace. The qualitative ordering that matters for a
practitioner --- ``EC2 $\sim$60\,s, Fargate $>$180\,s, Lambda
effectively~0\,s'' --- is most clearly read from
\cref{fig:elasticity-trace} directly.

\subsection{Cold-start distribution on Lambda}
\label{sec:results-cold}

\Cref{fig:cold} is the ECDF of latency for the cold-start probe (twelve
sequential requests with 60-second gaps). Crucially, all twelve samples
lie inside a narrow 28--48\,ms band --- \emph{warm container territory}
--- confirming a well-documented property of Lambda's platform: inter-request
gaps on the order of one minute are insufficient to reclaim an idle
execution environment. The single unambiguous cold start we recorded is
the first invocation after deployment, which we measured at 4.3\,s
end-to-end via \texttt{curl}. A proper cold-start characterisation study
needs gaps of roughly 5--15\,minutes; we flag this as future work and
note that the effect is well-characterised elsewhere in the literature
(\cite{peeking-serverless}, \cite{mcgrath-faas}).

\subsection{Cost-performance Pareto and break-even}
\label{sec:results-cost}

\Cref{fig:cost} evaluates \cref{eq:cost} at the measured per-request
execution times. The crossover between Lambda and the two
provisioned-capacity models converges at roughly \textbf{20\,RPS} of
sustained traffic: below that rate, Lambda's no-idle billing is cheapest
because EC2 and Fargate both pay for idle capacity around the clock;
above it, a single provisioned instance or task amortises across enough
requests that the flat hourly rate wins. The exact crossover depends on
three levers that our Terraform variables expose:
instance/task size, target utilisation before scale-out, and
$\overline{t}$. In the Pareto view of \cref{fig:pareto}, Lambda is
strictly dominant at 50\,RPS, offering both the lower p95 latency and
the lower cost per million requests. EC2 and Fargate are Pareto-dominated
in our measured regime, although this ordering would invert above
several hundred sustained RPS as the provisioned-capacity curves flatten
and the Lambda cost curve keeps growing linearly in $\rho$.
