\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Comparative measurements of cloud compute paradigms have been a steady
research thread for nearly a decade. Wang et al.~\cite{peeking-serverless}
characterised the runtime behaviour of major FaaS providers,
exposing the wide variation in cold-start times across vendors and
runtime sizes. McGrath and Brenner~\cite{mcgrath-faas} compared early
serverless platforms and proposed a hybrid scheduler that pre-warms
containers based on a workload predictor; their cold-start results are
qualitatively consistent with what we measured for the 2026
generation of AWS Lambda.

Eismann et al.~\cite{eismann2021review} systematise serverless
performance studies and identify a recurring weakness in the
literature: the comparison points are rarely identical applications,
which leaves any cross-paradigm conclusions vulnerable to
implementation differences. Our use of one OCI image across three
runtimes via the AWS Lambda Web Adapter~\cite{lambda-web-adapter} is a
direct response to this critique.

For cost analysis, Adzic and Chatley~\cite{adzic-serverless} examined
the conditions under which serverless wins over EC2 for production
workloads at MindMup; their break-even RPS is in the same order of
magnitude as ours but for a Java workload with very different
$\overline{t}$. Closer to our exact configuration, Cordingly et
al.~\cite{cordingly2020lambda} ran a measurement study of Lambda's
billing model and the impact of memory selection, which informed our
decision to run the headline configuration at 1\,GB rather than the
128\,MB minimum.
